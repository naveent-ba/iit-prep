# INORGANIC CHEMISTRY
## The Periodic Architecture — Coulomb's Law in Action

---

## PAGE 1 — The Core Insight

Inorganic Chemistry is **NOT memorization**.

It is the study of how **Charge (q)** and **Size (r)** determine everything.

### The Master Equation

$$F \propto \frac{q_1 \times q_2}{r^2}$$

This single equation explains:
- Why F is more electronegative than I
- Why Li⁺ has the highest hydration enthalpy
- Why MgO has a higher lattice energy than NaCl
- Why HF is a weak acid but HI is strong

> **The Invariant: Smaller atoms with higher charges form stronger interactions.**

### The Strategy

1. Don't memorize isolated facts
2. Understand the TREND
3. Memorize only the EXCEPTIONS (and know WHY they're exceptions)

## PAGE 2 — Periodic Trends: The Foundation

### The Two Forces

| Force | Effect | Direction |
|:------|:-------|:----------|
| Nuclear charge (Z) | Pulls electrons in | Increases → |
| Shielding | Blocks nuclear pull | Increases ↓ |
| Distance (shells) | Weakens attraction | Increases ↓ |

**Effective Nuclear Charge:**
$$Z_{eff} = Z - \sigma$$

Where σ = shielding constant

### The Master Trend Table

| Property | Across Period (→) | Down Group (↓) | Why |
|:---------|:------------------|:---------------|:----|
| Atomic radius | Decreases | Increases | More protons pull in (→), more shells push out (↓) |
| Ionization energy | Increases | Decreases | Smaller = harder to remove |
| Electron affinity | Increases (generally) | Decreases | Smaller = stronger attraction |
| Electronegativity | Increases | Decreases | Smaller = pulls harder |
| Metallic character | Decreases | Increases | Easier to lose electrons |

> **All trends come from the same source: Zeff/r ratio.**

## PAGE 3 — The Exceptions (And Why They Exist)

### Exception 1: Half-Filled and Fully-Filled Stability

**Extra stable configurations:**
- Half-filled: d⁵ (Cr, Mn) and p³ (N, P)
- Fully-filled: d¹⁰ (Cu, Zn) and p⁶ (Noble gases)

**Consequence:**
- Cr is [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (not 3d⁴ 4s²)
- Cu is [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (not 3d⁹ 4s²)

**IE Anomaly:**
- IE₁(N) > IE₁(O) because N has stable p³
- IE₁(P) > IE₁(S) for same reason

### Exception 2: d-Block Contraction

The d-orbitals shield poorly → increased Zeff for elements after d-block.

**Consequence:**
- Ga is similar in size to Al (not larger as expected)
- Post-transition metals are smaller than predicted

### Exception 3: Lanthanide Contraction

f-orbitals shield even more poorly than d-orbitals.

**Consequence:**
- Zr and Hf have nearly identical radii
- 5d elements have similar properties to 4d elements

## PAGE 4 — Ionization Energy: The Details

### Successive IE Pattern

$$IE_1 < IE_2 < IE_3 < ...$$

**Big jump when:** Core electrons are being removed.

Example for Na: IE₁ = 496, IE₂ = 4562 kJ/mol (huge jump!)

### Predicting Jumps

| Element | Valence electrons | Big jump after |
|:--------|:------------------|:---------------|
| Na | 1 | IE₁ |
| Mg | 2 | IE₂ |
| Al | 3 | IE₃ |

### The Anomalies

| Comparison | Expected | Actual | Reason |
|:-----------|:---------|:-------|:-------|
| IE(B) vs IE(Be) | B > Be | B < Be | B loses from 2p (farther, shielded by 2s) |
| IE(O) vs IE(N) | O > N | O < N | O has paired 2p electron (repulsion) |

## PAGE 5 — Electron Affinity

### Definition

Energy released when an atom gains an electron.

$$X(g) + e^- \to X^-(g) \quad \Delta H = -EA$$

### Trends

- Generally increases across period (more Zeff)
- Generally decreases down group (larger size)

### The Anomalies

| Comparison | Expected | Actual | Reason |
|:-----------|:---------|:-------|:-------|
| EA(F) vs EA(Cl) | F > Cl | F < Cl | F is too small → e⁻-e⁻ repulsion |
| EA(N) | Positive | Negative (≈0) | Adding to stable half-filled p³ |
| EA(Noble gases) | — | Highly negative | Fully filled → no room |

### Second Electron Affinity

Always positive (endothermic) — adding e⁻ to already negative ion requires energy.

Example: O⁻ + e⁻ → O²⁻ (ΔH > 0)

## PAGE 6 — Electronegativity

### Definition

Ability to attract shared electrons in a bond.

### Scales

| Scale | Method | Most electronegative |
|:------|:-------|:--------------------|
| Pauling | From bond energies | F = 4.0 |
| Mulliken | (IE + EA)/2 | F highest |
| Allred-Rochow | Force on valence e⁻ | F highest |

### Key Values (Pauling Scale)

| Element | χ |
|:--------|:--|
| F | 4.0 |
| O | 3.5 |
| Cl, N | 3.0 |
| Br | 2.8 |
| C, S | 2.5 |
| H | 2.1 |

### Applications

**Bond Polarity:**
$$\Delta \chi > 1.7 \to \text{Ionic}$$
$$\Delta \chi < 1.7 \to \text{Covalent (polar if Δχ > 0)}$$

**Inductive Effect:**
More electronegative atom → withdraws electrons through σ bonds

## PAGE 7 — Chemical Bonding: The Big Picture

### Types of Bonds

| Bond Type | Between | Driving Force |
|:----------|:--------|:--------------|
| Ionic | Metal + Non-metal | Electron transfer (Coulomb attraction) |
| Covalent | Non-metals | Electron sharing |
| Metallic | Metals | Sea of delocalized electrons |
| Coordinate | Donor + Acceptor | Lone pair donation |

### The Octet Rule

Atoms tend to achieve 8 electrons in valence shell (noble gas configuration).

**Exceptions:**
- Incomplete octet: BF₃ (6e⁻), BeCl₂ (4e⁻)
- Expanded octet: PCl₅ (10e⁻), SF₆ (12e⁻) — only for Period 3+

## PAGE 8 — Ionic Bonding

### Formation

Metal loses electrons → Cation
Non-metal gains electrons → Anion
Electrostatic attraction holds them together.

### Lattice Energy

Energy released when gaseous ions form solid crystal.

$$U \propto \frac{q^+ \times q^-}{r^+ + r^-}$$

### Factors Affecting Lattice Energy

| Factor | Effect on U |
|:-------|:------------|
| Higher charges | U increases |
| Smaller ions | U increases |

### Comparison

| Compound | Lattice Energy | Reason |
|:---------|:---------------|:-------|
| MgO > NaCl | Higher | 2+ and 2- vs 1+ and 1- |
| LiF > NaF | Higher | Li⁺ smaller than Na⁺ |
| NaF > NaCl | Higher | F⁻ smaller than Cl⁻ |

### Born-Haber Cycle

Uses Hess's Law to calculate lattice energy indirectly:

$$\Delta H_f = \Delta H_{sub} + IE + \frac{1}{2}D + EA + U$$

## PAGE 9 — Covalent Bonding: VSEPR Theory

### The Rule

> **Electron pairs (bonding and lone) repel each other and arrange to maximize distance.**

### Repulsion Order

$$\text{LP-LP} > \text{LP-BP} > \text{BP-BP}$$

(Lone pairs repel more because they're closer to nucleus)

### The VSEPR Table

| Total e⁻ pairs | Lone pairs | Shape | Example | Bond angle |
|:---------------|:-----------|:------|:--------|:-----------|
| 2 | 0 | Linear | BeCl₂ | 180° |
| 3 | 0 | Trigonal planar | BF₃ | 120° |
| 3 | 1 | Bent | SnCl₂ | <120° |
| 4 | 0 | Tetrahedral | CH₄ | 109.5° |
| 4 | 1 | Trigonal pyramidal | NH₃ | 107° |
| 4 | 2 | Bent | H₂O | 104.5° |
| 5 | 0 | Trigonal bipyramidal | PCl₅ | 90°, 120° |
| 5 | 1 | See-saw | SF₄ | — |
| 5 | 2 | T-shaped | ClF₃ | — |
| 5 | 3 | Linear | XeF₂ | 180° |
| 6 | 0 | Octahedral | SF₆ | 90° |
| 6 | 1 | Square pyramidal | BrF₅ | — |
| 6 | 2 | Square planar | XeF₄ | 90° |

### Lone Pair Position Rule

In 5 e⁻ pair systems: Lone pairs occupy **equatorial** positions (less repulsion).

## PAGE 10 — Hybridization

### The Concept

Atomic orbitals mix to form equivalent hybrid orbitals for bonding.

### Quick Formula

$$\text{Hybridization} = \frac{1}{2}(V + M - C + A)$$

Where:
- V = Valence electrons of central atom
- M = Monovalent atoms attached
- C = Positive charge
- A = Negative charge

### The Hybridization Table

| Hybrid | Orbitals mixed | Geometry | Angle | Example |
|:-------|:---------------|:---------|:------|:--------|
| sp | s + p | Linear | 180° | BeCl₂, C₂H₂ |
| sp² | s + 2p | Trigonal planar | 120° | BF₃, C₂H₄ |
| sp³ | s + 3p | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | CH₄, NH₃ |
| sp³d | s + 3p + d | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°, 120° | PCl₅ |
| sp³d² | s + 3p + 2d | Octahedral | 90° | SF₆ |

### Bond Strength Order

$$sp > sp^2 > sp^3$$

(More s-character = electrons closer to nucleus = stronger bond)

### Special Bonding Cases (Advanced Favorites)

**1. Back Bonding ($p\pi - p\pi$ or $p\pi - d\pi$)**

- **Condition:** One atom has a **Lone Pair**, the other has a **Vacant Orbital**.
- **Example:** $BF_3$. F donates lone pair to B's empty p-orbital.
- **Consequence:** B-F bond is shorter/stronger. Hybridization stays $sp^2$, but Lewis acidity **decreases**.

| Compound | Back Bonding? | Shape | Reason |
|:---------|:--------------|:------|:-------|
| $N(CH_3)_3$ | No | Pyramidal ($sp^3$) | No vacant orbital on C |
| $N(SiH_3)_3$ | Yes | Planar ($sp^2$) | N lone pair → Si empty d-orbital |

> **The Trap:** $N(SiH_3)_3$ is planar due to back bonding into Si d-orbital!

**2. Bridge Bonding (Banana Bond / 3c-2e)**

- **Example:** Diborane ($B_2H_6$)
- **Structure:** 3-center-2-electron bond (3c-2e)
- **Hybridization:** Boron is $sp^3$ (even though it appears to have only 3 bonds)
- **Geometry:** 4 Terminal H atoms in plane; 2 Bridge H atoms perpendicular

```
       H       H
        \     /
         B---B      (Bridge H atoms above and below plane)
        /     \
       H       H
```

> **Key:** Each bridge bond uses 2 electrons shared among 3 atoms.

## PAGE 11 — Molecular Orbital Theory

### The Concept

Atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals that belong to the whole molecule.

### Types

| Type | Energy | Electron location |
|:-----|:-------|:------------------|
| Bonding (σ, π) | Lower than AO | Between nuclei |
| Antibonding (σ*, π*) | Higher than AO | Outside nuclei |

### Energy Order

**For O₂, F₂, Ne₂ (O and beyond):**
$$\sigma_{1s} < \sigma^*_{1s} < \sigma_{2s} < \sigma^*_{2s} < \sigma_{2p_z} < \pi_{2p_x} = \pi_{2p_y} < \pi^*_{2p_x} = \pi^*_{2p_y} < \sigma^*_{2p_z}$$

**For N₂, C₂, B₂ (before O):**
$$\sigma_{2p_z} \text{ is ABOVE } \pi_{2p}$$

### Bond Order

$$\text{Bond Order} = \frac{1}{2}(N_b - N_{ab})$$

Where Nb = bonding electrons, Nab = antibonding electrons

### Magnetic Properties

- Unpaired electrons → Paramagnetic
- All paired → Diamagnetic

**Example:** O₂ has 2 unpaired electrons in π* → Paramagnetic

## PAGE 12 — Hydrogen Bonding and Intermolecular Forces

### Types of Intermolecular Forces

| Force | Between | Strength |
|:------|:--------|:---------|
| Ion-dipole | Ion and polar molecule | Strong |
| Hydrogen bond | H bonded to F, O, N | Moderate |
| Dipole-dipole | Polar molecules | Moderate |
| London dispersion | All molecules | Weak (but additive) |

### Hydrogen Bonding

Requirements:
1. H attached to F, O, or N
2. Lone pair available on nearby F, O, or N

**Consequences:**
- Anomalously high BP for H₂O, HF, NH₃
- Ice floats on water
- DNA double helix structure

### Boiling Point Trends

| Factor | Effect on BP |
|:-------|:-------------|
| H-bonding present | Large increase |
| Higher molecular weight | Increase (more London forces) |
| More polar | Increase |
| Branching | Decrease (less surface area) |

## PAGE 14 — Crystal Field Theory

### The Concept

Ligands are treated as point charges that split the d-orbital energies.

### Octahedral Splitting

```
        ↑ Energy
        │
        │    ___  ___   (eg: dx²-y², dz²)
        │       Δₒ
        │    ___  ___  ___  (t₂g: dxy, dxz, dyz)
        │
```

- eg orbitals: Higher energy (point toward ligands)
- t₂g orbitals: Lower energy (point between ligands)

### Tetrahedral Splitting

**Inverted** compared to octahedral:
- t₂ orbitals: Higher energy
- e orbitals: Lower energy
- $\Delta_t = \frac{4}{9}\Delta_o$

### Square Planar Splitting

Common for d⁸ (Pt²⁺, Pd²⁺, Ni²⁺ with strong ligands):
$$d_{x^2-y^2} > d_{xy} > d_{z^2} > d_{xz} = d_{yz}$$

## PAGE 15 — Spectrochemical Series

### The Order (Weak → Strong Field)

$$I^- < Br^- < Cl^- < F^- < OH^- < H_2O < NH_3 < en < NO_2^- < CN^- < CO$$

**Mnemonic:** "I Bring Clothes For Our House, Always Neatly, Not Carelessly"

### High Spin vs Low Spin

| Δ vs P | Configuration | Spin |
|:-------|:--------------|:-----|
| Δ < P (weak field) | Electrons spread out | High spin |
| Δ > P (strong field) | Electrons pair up | Low spin |

Where P = pairing energy

### Magnetic Moment

$$\mu = \sqrt{n(n+2)} \text{ BM}$$

Where n = number of unpaired electrons

### Color of Complexes

- Absorbed wavelength corresponds to Δ
- Observed color is complementary

| Δ large | Absorbs red | Appears blue/violet |
| Δ small | Absorbs blue | Appears red/yellow |

## PAGE 16 — Isomerism in Coordination Compounds

### Structural Isomers

| Type | Definition | Example |
|:-----|:-----------|:--------|
| Ionization | Different ions in/out of coordination sphere | [Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄ vs [Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br |
| Hydrate | H₂O in/out of coordination sphere | [Cr(H₂O)₆]Cl₃ vs [Cr(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂·H₂O |
| Linkage | Same ligand, different donor atom | [Co(NH₃)₅NO₂]²⁺ (N-bonded) vs [Co(NH₃)₅ONO]²⁺ (O-bonded) |
| Coordination | Exchange of ligands between cation and anion | [Co(NH₃)₆][Cr(CN)₆] vs [Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆] |

### Stereoisomers

**Geometrical (cis-trans):**
- Cis: Same ligands on same side
- Trans: Same ligands on opposite sides
- Occurs in square planar (MA₂B₂) and octahedral (MA₄B₂)

**Optical:**
- Non-superimposable mirror images
- Common in octahedral [M(en)₃]

## PAGE 17 — p-Block Elements: Group Trends

### General Pattern

| Property | Down the group |
|:---------|:---------------|
| Metallic character | Increases |
| Ionization energy | Decreases |
| Electronegativity | Decreases |
| Oxidation state stability | Lower states more stable |

### The Inert Pair Effect

**Heavy p-block elements prefer oxidation states 2 less than group number.**

| Group | Light element | Heavy element |
|:------|:--------------|:--------------|
| 13 | Al: +3 stable | Tl: +1 stable |
| 14 | C: +4 stable | Pb: +2 stable |
| 15 | N: +5 possible | Bi: +3 stable |

**Reason:** 6s² electrons are reluctant to participate in bonding (relativistic contraction).

### Anomalous Behavior of First Element

First element of each group differs from others because:
1. Small size
2. No d-orbitals
3. High electronegativity

Examples: N vs other Group 15, O vs other Group 16

### Thermal Stability (Fajan's Rule Application)

**The Logic:**
- High Polarization (High Charge/Small Cation) = Covalent character = **Low Stability**
- Low Polarization (Low Charge/Large Cation) = Ionic character = **High Stability**

**Decomposition Patterns:**

**1. Carbonates ($MCO_3$):**

| Group | Behavior | Reason |
|:------|:---------|:-------|
| Group 1 (Na → Cs) | Stable to heat | Large cations, low polarization |
| Li₂CO₃ | Decomposes: $Li_2CO_3 \to Li_2O + CO_2$ | Small Li⁺ polarizes CO₃²⁻ |
| Group 2 (Be → Ba) | Stability **increases** down | BeCO₃ unstable, BaCO₃ stable |

**2. Nitrates ($MNO_3$):**

| Group | Products | Equation |
|:------|:---------|:---------|
| Group 1 (Na, K, Rb, Cs) | Nitrite + O₂ | $2MNO_3 \to 2MNO_2 + O_2$ |
| LiNO₃ and Group 2 | Oxide + NO₂ + O₂ | $4MNO_3 \to 2M_2O + 4NO_2 + O_2$ |

> **The Pattern:** Small cation = more polarization = more complete decomposition.

## PAGE 25 — d-Block: Important Compounds

### Potassium Dichromate (K₂Cr₂O₇)

**Preparation:**
$$4FeCr_2O_4 + 8Na_2CO_3 + 7O_2 \to 8Na_2CrO_4 + 2Fe_2O_3 + 8CO_2$$

**Acidification:**
$$2CrO_4^{2-} + 2H^+ \rightleftharpoons Cr_2O_7^{2-} + H_2O$$

**As oxidizing agent:**
$$Cr_2O_7^{2-} + 14H^+ + 6e^- \to 2Cr^{3+} + 7H_2O$$

### Potassium Permanganate (KMnO₄)

**In acidic medium (Mn: +7 → +2):**
$$MnO_4^- + 8H^+ + 5e^- \to Mn^{2+} + 4H_2O$$

**In neutral/weakly alkaline (Mn: +7 → +4):**
$$MnO_4^- + 2H_2O + 3e^- \to MnO_2 + 4OH^-$$

**In strongly alkaline (Mn: +7 → +6):**
$$MnO_4^- + e^- \to MnO_4^{2-}$$

## PAGE 26 — Metallurgy: Extraction Principles

### The Ellingham Diagram Principle

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$

- Lines slope upward because ΔS is negative (gas → solid oxide)
- Metal with lower line can reduce oxide of metal with higher line

### Reduction Methods

| Oxide Type | Reduction Method | Example |
|:-----------|:-----------------|:--------|
| Very reactive metals | Electrolysis of fused salt | Na, Al |
| Moderately reactive | Carbon/CO reduction | Fe, Zn |
| Less reactive | Self-reduction or heating | Cu, Hg |
| Noble metals | Already free in nature | Au, Pt |

### Key Processes

**Thermite Process:**
$$Fe_2O_3 + 2Al \to 2Fe + Al_2O_3$$
(Very exothermic, used for welding rails)

**Blast Furnace (Iron):**
$$Fe_2O_3 + 3CO \to 2Fe + 3CO_2$$

**Hall-Héroult (Aluminum):**
Electrolysis of Al₂O₃ dissolved in molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆)

## PAGE 27 — Qualitative Analysis: Logic Over Memory

Most students memorize colors. You will look for **Polarization**.

### Solubility Rules (The Competition: Lattice vs Hydration)

**Small Anions ($F^-, OH^-, O^{2-}$):** Lattice energy dominates. Solubility depends on Cation size.
- Group 1: Soluble (Large cations)
- Group 2: Solubility **increases** down the group
  - Be(OH)₂ insoluble → Ba(OH)₂ soluble

**Large Anions ($SO_4^{2-}, CO_3^{2-}$):** Hydration energy dominates.
- Group 2: Solubility **decreases** down the group
  - BeSO₄ soluble → BaSO₄ insoluble

| Anion Size | Dominant Factor | Solubility Trend (down Group 2) |
|:-----------|:----------------|:--------------------------------|
| Small (OH⁻, F⁻) | Lattice Energy | Increases ↓ |
| Large (SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻) | Hydration Energy | Decreases ↓ |

### The Color Logic (Charge Transfer vs d-d)

**1. d-d Transition:** Needs unpaired d-electrons.
- Hydrated Cu²⁺ is blue; anhydrous CuSO₄ is white
- Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰) and Sc³⁺ (d⁰) are colorless

**2. Charge Transfer (L → M):** Electron jumps from Ligand to Metal.
- Responsible for **INTENSE** colors
- Examples: KMnO₄ (purple), K₂Cr₂O₇ (orange), CrO₄²⁻ (yellow)

**3. Polarization → Color Deepening:**

| Compound | Color | Reason |
|:---------|:------|:-------|
| AgCl | White | Low polarization |
| AgBr | Pale yellow | Medium polarization |
| AgI | Yellow | High polarization |

> **Higher polarization = Deeper color**

### Gas Evolution Logic (Confirmatory Tests)

| Ion | Test | Observation |
|:----|:-----|:------------|
| $CO_3^{2-}$ | Dilute acid | CO₂ evolved → Lime water turns milky |
| $S^{2-}$ | Dilute acid | H₂S (rotten egg) → Lead acetate paper turns black |
| $NO_3^-$ | Heat + Cu turnings + H₂SO₄ | NO₂ brown gas evolved |
| $SO_3^{2-}$ | Dilute acid | SO₂ → Acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ turns green |
| $Cl^-$ | Conc. H₂SO₄ | HCl → White fumes with NH₃ |

## PAGE 28 — Common Traps and Fixes

### Periodic Trends Traps

| Trap | Fix |
|:-----|:----|
| "Noble gases have no EA" | They have highly negative EA (unfavorable) |
| "F has highest EA" | Cl has higher EA than F (F is too small) |
| "IE always increases across period" | Anomalies at N, O due to stability factors |

### Bonding Traps

| Trap | Fix |
|:-----|:----|
| "More bonds = more stable" | Lone pair repulsion matters too |
| "CO₂ has double bonds so it's bent" | Linear because no lone pairs on C |
| "All d⁸ are square planar" | Only with strong field ligands |

### Coordination Chemistry Traps

| Trap | Fix |
|:-----|:----|
| "High oxidation = high spin" | Depends on ligand field strength |
| "Tetrahedral complexes are low spin" | Almost never—Δt is too small |
| "More ligands = higher CN" | Bidentate ligands count each donor |

## PAGE 29 — The Inorganic Chemistry Algorithm

```
INORGANIC PROBLEM
│
├── Periodic Trend question?
│   └── Use Coulomb's Law logic (q/r²)
│       ├── Smaller = stronger pull
│       └── Higher charge = stronger interaction
│
├── Structure/Shape question?
│   └── Use VSEPR
│       ├── Count electron pairs
│       ├── Identify lone pairs
│       └── Predict geometry
│
├── Bonding question?
│   └── Determine type
│       ├── ΔEN > 1.7 → Ionic
│       ├── ΔEN < 1.7 → Covalent
│       └── Metal-Metal → Metallic
│
├── Coordination compound?
│   └── Crystal Field Theory
│       ├── Identify geometry
│       ├── Check spectrochemical series
│       └── Predict color/magnetism
│
└── Exception appears?
    └── Check secondary factors
        ├── Half-filled stability
        ├── d-block contraction
        └── Inert pair effect
```

> **Trend first. Exception second. Always ask WHY.**
